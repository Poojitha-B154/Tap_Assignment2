Introduction

For over half a century, Earth Day has stood as a symbol of our collective responsibility to protect the planet we call home. First observed on April 22, 1970, Earth Day emerged at a pivotal moment in history, as people across the United States rallied to address the visible and growing threats of pollution, deforestation, oil spills, and habitat destruction. The event marked the birth of the modern environmental movement and laid the groundwork for landmark legislation such as the Clean Air Act, Clean Water Act, and the Endangered Species Act. Since then, Earth Day has grown into the largest secular civic event in the world, observed by over a billion people in more than 190 countries — a testament to the shared nature of our environmental challenges and the universal desire for a healthier planet.

Earth Day is more than just a date on the calendar — it is a global call to action, a moment to pause and recognize the profound interconnectedness between human activity and the ecosystems that sustain us. As climate change accelerates, biodiversity declines, and resource scarcity intensifies, the relevance of Earth Day has never been more urgent. It’s a time for education, activism, and reflection — but also for celebration of the Earth’s beauty and resilience.

It’s a time for many to get outside to our beautiful parks and hike our natural places. It’s an occasion to tend to gardens that will soon be bursting with flowering plants and vegetables. But if Earth Day is an opportunity to appreciate the natural world, it is also a moment to take stock of threats it faces and what we can do to protect it. For those of us in the conservation community, that often comes in the form of officially designated “protected areas” designed to ensure the sustainable use of natural resources in a manner that protects species richness and habitat, while allowing resident Indigenous Peoples and local communities to continue to occupy and secure their traditional territories while benefiting from new livelihood opportunities.

In an increasingly interconnected world, the United States’ commitment to the protection of nature across the globe is not merely an act of altruism but a strategic imperative that safeguards national security and promotes economic prosperity. Grounded in the principles of natural security, this commitment also addresses the intricate linkages between environmental conservation and geopolitical stability.

Concepts

The concept of natural security is grounded in the recognition that environmental degradation is not only an ecological concern, but also a profound security threat with direct implications for global stability and human well-being. It holds that the destruction of ecosystems—through activities such as deforestation, illegal mining, unsustainable land use, wildlife trafficking, climate change, and water scarcity—can act as force multipliers for conflict, forced migration, poverty, and public health crises. For instance, dwindling freshwater supplies can intensify tensions between communities or nations, while poor land management can lead to soil erosion and crop failure, undermining food security and driving displacement.

In fragile regions, these environmental stressors often interact with existing socio-political tensions, leading to increased risk of violence and instability. The illegal wildlife trade, for example, is not only a conservation crisis—it is a transnational crime that generates billions of dollars annually, financing criminal networks, terrorist organizations, and extremist groups. These actors undermine governance, weaken border security, and threaten U.S. and allied interests, especially in regions of strategic importance such as sub-Saharan Africa, Southeast Asia, and Central America.

By investing in protected areas, the U.S. and its partners aim to address these threats at their ecological root—protecting biodiversity and ecosystems that, when healthy, provide resilience against climate impacts, promote economic opportunities through eco-tourism and sustainable livelihoods, and reduce incentives for illegal exploitation of resources. These protected zones, when managed effectively, can serve as buffers against ecological collapse and as platforms for peacebuilding in post-conflict areas. Unfortunately, in far too many cases, these areas become “paper parks”—places where a government has made commitments to conservation on paper, but lacks the institutional capacity, political will, or financial resources to enforce those protections on the ground. Local communities based near or within these parks often face poverty, land insecurity, or exclusion from decision-making processes, and therefore lack both the incentives and support needed to help steward the land. Without inclusive governance and long-term funding, the promise of natural security risks becoming a missed opportunity, leaving ecosystems vulnerable and communities unprotected.

Use Cases

In 2024, to address that challenge while recognizing the importance of conservation, Congress passed the U. Foundation for International Conservation Act. This bipartisan legislation established a groundbreaking public-private partnership to support global conservation and economic development, authorizing up to $100 million in federal funding annually, matched 2:1 by non-U.S. government funding.

The foundation aims to protect endangered wildlife, support conservation efforts worldwide, and, by extension, enhance global stability while combating illicit activities that threaten U.S. interests. The nonpartisan nature of international conservation funding is exemplified by the collaborative efforts of U.S. Senators Lindsey Graham (R-SC) and Chris Coons (D-DE) in championing the bill, which was enacted in December 2024.

Senator Graham highlights the strategic advantages provided by conservation efforts when he notes, “The U.S. Foundation for International Conservation is a win-win in that the bill leverages private capital while supporting the long-term protection of critical landscapes around the world. Investments in conservation lead to food security and regional stability.”

Sen. Graham’s comment underscores the recognition across party lines that international conservation funding is integral to U.S. national security and global leadership. By maintaining and enhancing such assistance we strategically position our nation to address global challenges that have direct implications for domestic stability and prosperity. Marco Rubio’s support for the law during his confirmation hearings to become U.S. Secretary of State affirms that idea. As intact areas with ecological integrity—that is, areas with a full array of species in very healthy numbers and with its ecosystem functioning as it should—become degraded, they are denuded of their trees. Fish and wildlife are no longer abundant. For local people, these areas become unlivable, and you start to see increased pressure on out-migration, creating more pressure on neighboring countries.

That creates conditions of instability that gives rise to rebel groups and crippling insurgencies, as you find in Central Africa and parts of Latin America today. The establishment of the US Foundation for International Conservation reinforces the nation’s longstanding commitment to conservation as a means of securing a safer, healthier planet and demonstrating by example the power of democratic governance and American values.

It also enables us to benefit from the innumerable services that nature provides for people across the globe—from wetlands buffering the impacts of storms of growing intensity to the multitude of medicines derived from nature. To take just one example, the chemical vinblastine, found in Madagascar’s rosy periwinkle plant, has been critical to the treatment of lymphomas and testicular, breast, bladder, and lung cancers. When grounded in the principles of natural security, conservation addresses the complex interplay between environmental conservation and geopolitical stability. Moreover, as a public-private partnership, it does so without the entire burden falling on American taxpayers.

Conclusion

This Earth Day, as we take a pause to take in the beauty of nature with a short walk in, or drive from, our respective cities, towns, suburbs, and rural areas, let’s save a thought for the expansive and as-yet unsullied landscapes and marine areas whose protection contributes to the peace and security of countless regions across the planet that provide us with so many blessings. These intact ecosystems are not only home to diverse species and cultural heritage — they are vital carbon sinks, climate buffers, and natural defense systems against rising sea levels, extreme weather, and resource conflict.

Let us remember that the protection of nature is not a luxury or symbolic gesture — it is an investment in our shared future. Whether it’s a coral reef in the Pacific safeguarding fisheries and coastal livelihoods, or a rainforest in the Congo Basin stabilizing the global climate, these places matter. They connect us all.

As individuals, communities, and nations, we must move beyond awareness toward accountability and action — supporting policies, partnerships, and initiatives that ensure protected areas are not just lines on a map, but living landscapes where nature thrives, local communities flourish, and future generations can continue to draw strength, inspiration, and sustenance from the natural world.

Earth Day is not just about celebration — it is also about commitment. A commitment to care, to conserve, and to act boldly and collectively to ensure that the beauty we admire today does not become a memory tomorrow. As global citizens, we must recognize that Earth’s ecosystems do not respect political boundaries. The health of a wetland in South America, a glacier in the Arctic, or a mangrove forest in Southeast Asia directly impacts the health of our planet — and our future. On this Earth Day, let us renew our shared responsibility to act across borders, build climate resilience, and protect the natural heritage that sustains us all. This Earth Day reminds us not only of nature’s beauty, but of its fragility — and our responsibility. Let us recognize conservation as both a moral and strategic imperative, one that demands local empowerment, global cooperation, and unwavering resolve. By acting today, we honor not only the planet, but the generations yet to come.
